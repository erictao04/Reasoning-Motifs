# Reasoning Motifs Under Outcome-Only Supervision: A Current-Status Workshop Report on Mixed-Outcome Math Traces

## Abstract
Large language model (LLM) reasoning is increasingly evaluated and trained through final-answer correctness, yet answer accuracy alone hides substantial variation in the quality of intermediate reasoning. This report studies whether recurring tokenized reasoning motifs can recover useful process-level signals from outcome-labeled traces alone. We analyze a corpus of math reasoning traces generated by a single base model, `Qwen/Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct-Turbo`, and compare two independent LLM-based tokenizations of the same underlying traces: a `gpt-oss-120b` tokenization and a `DeepSeek-V3.1` tokenization. Our focus is mixed-outcome settings, where the same question receives both correct and incorrect sampled traces. We ask three questions: (1) do local motifs predict correctness on held-out questions, (2) do early-prefix motifs provide useful early-warning signal, and (3) are motif associations robust across tokenizers? The current evidence is mixed. Motif-based predictors achieve only weak held-out performance within tokenization, and they do not outperform a simple trace-length baseline in question-local ranking. However, some motif associations are moderately stable across tokenizations of the same traces, and cross-tokenization transfer yields nontrivial global AUC. The resulting picture is not that local motifs already explain success and failure, but that they may capture weak, representation-robust process tendencies. We argue that this is still useful: it supports a narrower claim about outcome-only process discovery while also clarifying the limits of local motif abstractions.

## 1. Introduction
Reasoning models do not produce a single deterministic chain of thought. For many questions, especially on mathematical reasoning tasks, the same underlying model can generate multiple distinct trajectories, some correct and some incorrect. This makes final-answer accuracy an incomplete description of model behavior. A model may be capable of solving a problem but unreliable in how it gets there.

This motivates a process-level view of reasoning traces. Recent work on process supervision and process reward models has shown that intermediate reasoning quality matters, both for capability and for oversight. OpenAI's "Let's Verify Step by Step" showed that step-level supervision can outperform pure outcome supervision on MATH and released the PRM800K dataset to support research on process-level training signals. At the same time, work such as ReasonEval and ProcessBench argues that answer accuracy alone hides logical errors, redundant reasoning, and process failures that only become visible when intermediate steps are evaluated directly. More recent verifier work such as ThinkPRM and ReasonFlux-PRM continues this trend by trying to recover dense process-level signals at lower labeling cost.

This project asks a smaller but still important question: can we extract useful process-level evidence without human step labels, using only repeated outcome-labeled traces? Our working hypothesis is that if the same question yields both successful and failed trajectories, then recurring local reasoning motifs may separate productive reasoning from failure-prone reasoning. If true, motif mining could provide an inexpensive, interpretable bridge between answer-level supervision and richer process analysis.

The current report is intentionally a "status quo" paper rather than a polished final submission. It does not assume the method already works strongly. Instead, it treats the present experimental state honestly: some qualitative motifs look plausible and some signals survive cross-tokenization, but the main held-out predictive story remains weak. In our view, this is still worth reporting. A workshop paper can be valuable not only by presenting a strong positive result, but also by identifying where a promising direction appears brittle, what kinds of robustness are already present, and what questions remain open.

Our central takeaway is therefore modest. Under the current setup, local tokenized motifs are not yet sufficient to robustly explain trace-level correctness. However, they do appear to capture weak, interpretable tendencies that persist across independent tokenizations of the same traces. This supports a narrower contribution: outcome-only motif mining can surface candidate process signals, but current local motif abstractions are not yet strong enough to serve as a complete success/failure explanation layer.

We study two tokenized views of this same corpus:

- [tokenizer/clean_expanded_pool_s100_seed73_qwen25_7b_hot30_tokenized_gpt-oss-120b_0.csv](/Users/czqz/440-project/Reasoning-Motifs/tokenizer/clean_expanded_pool_s100_seed73_qwen25_7b_hot30_tokenized_gpt-oss-120b_0.csv)
- [tokenizer/clean_expanded_pool_s100_seed73_qwen25_7b_hot30_tokenized_DeepSeek-V3.1_0.csv](/Users/czqz/440-project/Reasoning-Motifs/tokenizer/clean_expanded_pool_s100_seed73_qwen25_7b_hot30_tokenized_DeepSeek-V3.1_0.csv)

These files are not different generators; they are different LLM-based tokenizations of the same underlying reasoning traces. This distinction matters. Any "transfer" claim here should be interpreted as cross-tokenization robustness, not cross-model generalization.

After cleaning, the `gpt-oss` tokenization contains 2,683 traces and the `DeepSeek` tokenization contains 2,339 traces. We also consider mixed-outcome subsets, where a question has both correct and incorrect traces in the given tokenization. These contain 2,415 traces for `gpt-oss` and 1,784 traces for `DeepSeek`.

This mixed-outcome slice is especially important for the intended research story. Global success/failure comparisons can be confounded by question difficulty: some motifs may correlate with hard question types rather than with reasoning quality per se. Mixed-outcome questions are cleaner because they ask a more local question: holding the prompt fixed, what changes across successful and failed trajectories?

## 3. Method
### 3.1 Motif representation
Each tokenized reasoning trace is represented as a sequence of canonical reasoning-action tokens, such as `analyze`, `derive-intermediate`, `solve`, `conclude`, `case-split`, or `backtrack`. From each sequence we extract unique contiguous motifs of length 1 to 3. The use of document-level support means a motif counts at most once per trace rather than by raw within-trace frequency.

### 3.2 Predictor
To test whether motifs carry useful signal, we train a simple Bernoulli Naive Bayes-style classifier over motif presence. Features are retained only if they appear at least 12 times in the training split. The goal of this model is not to maximize benchmark performance but to provide a simple, interpretable probe: if local motifs are genuinely informative, even a lightweight presence-based model should recover some signal.

### 3.3 Baseline
We compare against a trace-length baseline. This is important because reasoning studies often conflate process quality with verbosity. The repository already contains evidence that raw length effects are subtle under question-controlled shuffling, but length remains a useful sanity baseline for ranking traces.

### 3.4 Experiments
We run three families of experiments using [scripts/run_paper_experiments.py](/Users/czqz/440-project/Reasoning-Motifs/scripts/run_paper_experiments.py):

1. Held-out question prediction. We use 5-fold question-level splits so that test questions are never seen during training. This is the main check for whether motif signals generalize beyond memorizing question identity.
2. Early-prefix prediction. We restrict traces to the first 25%, 50%, 75%, or 100% of tokens to test whether motifs provide useful early-warning signals.
3. Cross-tokenization robustness. We train on motif weights from one tokenization and evaluate on the other tokenization of the same underlying traces. This is not cross-model transfer; it is a representation-robustness test.

In addition to global AUC and balanced accuracy, we compute question-local AUC by ranking correct and incorrect traces within each question and averaging over mixed-outcome questions. This metric is especially relevant because the motivating story is fundamentally local: motifs should ideally help distinguish successful and failed reasoning trajectories for the same prompt.

## 4. Results
### 4.1 Held-out question prediction is weak
The strongest positive story we might have hoped for is that local motifs predict correctness on held-out questions. Under the current setup, they do not do so strongly.

On the full `gpt-oss` tokenization, motif prediction reaches AUC 0.5070, essentially near chance. On the mixed-outcome subset, the AUC rises only to 0.5403. On the `DeepSeek` tokenization the full and mixed-question AUCs are 0.5500 and 0.5569 respectively. These are weak effects. They suggest some nonzero signal, but not a strong predictive story.

The question-local view is also sobering. Motif question-local AUC is 0.5422 on `gpt-oss` full, 0.5559 on `gpt-oss` mixed, 0.5123 on `DeepSeek` full, and 0.5328 on `DeepSeek` mixed. All of these are modest. More importantly, the length baseline achieves higher question-local AUC in every case, ranging from 0.6117 to 0.6549. Thus, under the current motif representation, local motifs do not yet beat a simple length-based ranking signal on within-question separation.

This matters for the paper's main thesis. It means we cannot currently claim that mined motifs robustly explain why one trace succeeds and another fails on the same prompt. At best, they provide weak additional structure.

### 4.2 Early-prefix motifs do not yet give a strong early-warning signal
If motifs are to matter for verifier design or process oversight, they should ideally show up early enough to predict outcome before the trace finishes. We therefore evaluate prefix fractions from 0.25 to 1.0.

Here too the evidence is mixed and mostly weak. On `gpt-oss`, motif AUC at the 25% prefix is 0.5495, but it drops to 0.5020 at 50%, 0.4829 at 75%, and 0.5070 at full length. On `DeepSeek`, motif AUC is 0.5393 at 25% but hovers around 0.50 to 0.55 thereafter. Question-local results are similarly modest. The main lesson is not that early motifs are useless, but that the current local motif definition does not yet yield a stable early-warning system.

### 4.3 Cross-tokenization robustness is more encouraging
The most interesting positive result is the cross-tokenization robustness test. Training on `gpt-oss` tokenized motifs and testing on `DeepSeek` tokenized motifs yields global AUC 0.5976. The reverse direction yields AUC 0.5622. These are noticeably stronger than the weakest within-tokenization held-out results and suggest that some motif-level associations survive independent tokenizations of the same traces.

However, this result must be interpreted carefully. Because the underlying examples are the same traces, this is not evidence that motifs generalize across different generator models. It is evidence that some motif associations are robust to different semantic abstraction schemes applied to the same reasoning corpus. This is still useful, but it is a smaller claim.

Question-local transfer remains weak. The cross-tokenization motif question-local AUCs are 0.5351 and 0.4937, both below the corresponding length question-local baselines. So the robustness story is stronger at the level of global representation consistency than at the level of fine-grained within-question success/failure ranking.

### 4.4 Some motifs are semantically plausible and moderately stable
Despite weak predictive performance, the learned motifs are not obviously meaningless. The top shared success-leaning motifs across tokenizations include `solve conclude`, `solve`, `derive-intermediate solve`, `simplify isolate`, and `isolate compute`. The top shared failure-leaning motifs include `apply-formula case-split`, `backtrack rewrite`, `compute backtrack`, and `apply-formula compute analyze`.

Across the 357 shared features between the two tokenizations, sign agreement is 55.46%, with 96 shared success motifs and 102 shared failure motifs. This is not overwhelming agreement, but it is enough to suggest that some local reasoning tendencies survive tokenization differences. Qualitatively, the success motifs appear more resolution-oriented, while the failure motifs more often suggest branching, recovery, or drift. The caution is that semantic plausibility alone is not enough; the quantitative signal remains limited.

## 5. Discussion
### 5.1 Do the motifs support a success story?
Only partially. Under the current status quo, the motifs support a semantic success story more than a statistical one. Some motifs do look like plausible markers of productive reasoning, and some failure motifs look like plausible markers of detours or instability. But the current classifier results are too weak to claim that local motifs strongly explain trace-level success or failure.

This distinction is important. A motif such as `solve conclude` may be associated with correct traces not because it causes success, but because it is a visible symptom of an already-successful trajectory. Similarly, a motif such as `backtrack rewrite` may appear in failed traces not because recovery is intrinsically bad, but because models invoke recovery when they are already off track.

### 5.2 Why might the signal be weak?
Several explanations are plausible.

First, the tokenization may be too lossy. Two traces with very different mathematical quality can collapse into the same local token pattern if the abstraction omits what quantity was being tracked or what formula was applied.

Second, the important signal may be nonlocal. Success on multi-step math problems often depends on long-range consistency: choosing the right representation early, preserving variable meaning, or maintaining the correct subgoal. Local 1- to 3-token motifs may simply be too small to capture this structure.

Third, motifs may be confounded with problem families. Some patterns, such as `case-split`, may appear more often on inherently difficult question types. If so, they can look failure-associated without being intrinsically bad reasoning moves.

Fourth, success and failure are both heterogeneous. There may be many different good strategies and many different bad ones. In that setting, any one local motif will carry only weak signal.

Finally, externalized reasoning is not guaranteed to be fully faithful to the model's internal computation. Prior work from Anthropic and others suggests that chain-of-thought can be behaviorally useful without fully exposing the true causal path to the answer. If so, local motifs in the visible trace may only weakly approximate the latent reasoning state that actually determines correctness.

## 6. Related Work
This report sits between several active lines of research.

Process supervision and PRMs. OpenAI's "Let's Verify Step by Step" showed that step-level supervision can outperform outcome-only supervision on mathematical reasoning and released PRM800K to support downstream research. More recent verifier work such as ThinkPRM and ReasonFlux-PRM continues to explore how dense or generative process-level rewards can improve reasoning and test-time selection.

Reasoning evaluation beyond accuracy. ReasonEval argues that final-answer accuracy can hide logical errors and redundant steps, while ProcessBench studies identification of process errors in mathematical reasoning. Our work shares this motivation, but asks a cheaper question: what can be extracted from repeated outcome-labeled traces without manual step annotations?

Outcome-only reasoning training. DeepSeek-R1 and follow-up RLVR work emphasize that answer-level rewards can incentivize stronger reasoning behavior. Our work is relevant here because outcome-only training creates a need for post hoc methods that explain what kinds of visible reasoning patterns are actually being selected.

Chain-of-thought faithfulness. Anthropic's work on faithfulness and monitorability warns against equating visible chain-of-thought with true internal reasoning. This is a direct caution for the present project: our motifs should be interpreted as properties of externalized reasoning behavior, not as direct readouts of hidden cognition.

Test-time scaling and multi-path reasoning. Self-consistency and Tree of Thoughts treat multiple reasoning paths as a resource for answer selection or search. Our mixed-outcome framing is closely related, but instead of using multiple traces to choose an answer, we use them to study what structural regularities recur in success and failure.

## 7. Conclusion
Under the current status quo, motif mining offers a promising but still limited bridge between answer-level supervision and process-level reasoning analysis. The present evidence does not support a strong claim that local motifs robustly explain success and failure on held-out questions. Within-tokenization predictive performance is weak, and question-local motif ranking does not yet beat a simple length baseline.

At the same time, the method is not empty. Some motifs are semantically interpretable, and some associations are moderately robust across independent tokenizations of the same underlying traces. This supports a narrower claim that outcome-only motif mining can surface weak, representation-robust process signals from mixed-outcome trace distributions.

For a workshop paper, we believe this is already valuable. The current report clarifies both what works and what does not. It suggests that local symbolic motifs are not sufficient on their own, but may still be useful as one component in a richer process-analysis stack that incorporates question-local controls, longer-range structure, and stronger baselines. The next stage of this project should therefore aim not merely to mine more motifs, but to determine what additional structure is required for motifs to become genuinely explanatory rather than merely suggestive.
